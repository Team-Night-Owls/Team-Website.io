What does it do?

Natural Language Processing (NLP) refers to a branch of artificial intelligence that currently equips machines with the ability to read, understand, and derive meaning from human all languages. It combines the field of linguistics and computer science to decipher language structure guidelines and to make models which can comprehend, break down and separate significant details from text and speech. Humans interact with each other on a daily basis, for example over social media platforms, transferring large quantities of freely available data to each other. This data is extremely useful for NLP in understanding human behaviors and the habits of its customers.

Most recently, data analysts tend to use the entirety of this data to give machines the ability to mimic human communication behaviors. This currently saves companies a significant amount of man power and time, as they no longer need to have a person present at the other end of the phone to be able to assist a customer with an issue. NLP can be applied every day in many different situations, for example when you are unsure of the spelling of a certain word, Autocorrect steps in to resolve this issue in a very convenient manner. Additionally, if a term paper was flagged for potential plagiarism, there are a vast number of online plagiarism checkers that can check for matches with your documents from all over the web.

Future directions of this field of technology include:
- Enhanced interpretability (Zhang et al., 2021): this development is becoming increasingly important as technology generates more complex models and programs. Interpretability allows computers to effectively analyse and evaluate models for specific purposes.
- Emotional detection and conveyance (Strapparava, 2016): the ability to interpret and convey emotion can facilitate the human connection aspect of this technology. As the basic purpose of NLP is to derive meaning in human communication, the ability to evaluate syntactic and semantic features will be enhanced with this additional layer of consideration. For example, emotions can impact on more subtle aspects of communication, such as implied meanings and sarcasm.
- Clinical applications in medicine (Nehme & Feldman, 2020): This is an exciting direction that will likely develop a major role in different medical industries. This paper illustrated an early example, where NLP is being used to facilitate the translation of research to clinic practice in the field of gastroenterology, in particular for complex surgical procedures.

There are several important advances in technology that have enabled NLP in the past, and will continue to support its evolution:
- Word-sense disambiguation – being able to discern the sense of a word, as it is used in a particular sentence. This has implications for discourse and the relevance of search engines.
- Part of speech tagging Parsing – to analyse the grammar, speech and syntactic relations in a sentence, as well as being able to describe a word in the sentence.
- Spam detection – a series of filters are used to generate a binary classification of emails as relevant or spam, and consequently being able to direct them into a spam folder. 
- Sentiment detection – this form of analysis is a foundational component of natural language processing, and enables a computer to be able to identify and extract opinions within any given text.
- Named entity recognition – this process enables a computer to detect any important names (for people, organisations, brands etc) that are mentioned in a text, and thus be able to sort additional information into relevant and extraneous streams.
- Coreference resolution – Finding all expressions that refer to the same entity in a string of text (e.g., identifying the named subject and any further referents via the use of pronouns)
- Question-answering Information extraction – there are several approaches to this component, including knowledge based and information retrieval based processes. These paradigms allow a computer to analyse the question, retrieve any relevant information and generate appropriate answers
- Dialogue – these systems function to enable conversation with humans, as they employ one of various communication modes on both input and output channels
- Machine translation – being able to automatically convert one natural language into another, whilst avoiding any deviations in the semantics features of the text
- Paraphrasing and summarising technology – this is a discourse level of analysis, that requires in-depth analysis of text components and overall main ideas (Ferrari, 2018).

What does it do?

There are six (6) different levels of autonomy according to the Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) (“The 6 Levels”, 2019):
Level 0: Human driver is responsible for all aspects of driving.
Level 1: There is some driver assistance where the vehicle is capable of steering, acceleration, and breaking. However, the driver is still in control of all aspects of driving.
Level 2: The vehicle can perform steering and acceleration at the same time. However, the driver still needs to be present and aware in order to respond if needed.
Level 3: Similar to level 2, however the driver is not needed to monitor the situation. Level 3 still requires the driver to take back control if prompted.
Level 4: Similar to level 3, however no driver is required even while the vehicle is in automated mode.
Level 5: The automated vehicle is in control always and can resolve failures within the system. A steering wheel is not required in Level 5 vehicles.

As of 2021, a majority of vehicles that are currently on the road are only at level 1 or level 2 of automation (Dia, 2021). Technologies such as adaptive cruise control (Level 1) allows the vehicle to keep a safe distance from the car in front of them, however the human must continue to control the steering and breaking and remain alert as usual. Level 2 vehicles have this adaptive cruise control technology as well as autosteer, meaning multiple technologies are working together (“The 6 Levels”, 2019). Like Level 1, the driver will still need to remain focused and alert.

The jump between Level 2 automation and Level 3 automation is seemingly quite a difficult one. Back in 2019, Audi announced they would release a Level 3 autonomous vehicle featuring Traffic Jam Pilot. This would use a “lidar scanner with advanced sensor fusion and processing power” (“The 6 Levels”, 2019, para. 8). This would have meant that the driver could turn this on and essentially take their hands off the steering wheel. When the systems are unable to handle a situation, it will alert the driver and they will be able to take over control. Unfortunately, this was delayed as there was not enough legal framework to approve the functions of this Level 3 vehicle (Fung, 2020).

Even Level 4 vehicles are already being tested. In Arizona, a Level 4 taxi service was unveiled. These self-driving vehicles had been tested for over a year, across 10 million miles! Magna, a Canadian automotive supplier has developed the technology allowing for Level 4 automation, but only on highway and urban environments (“The 6 Levels”, 2019).

It is absolutely feasible that within the next few years, at least Level 3 autonomous vehicles will become more common on our roads. However, with this comes significant changes that are absolutely required to ensure the smooth transition to more autonomous systems.

In order for autonomous vehicles to become commonplace, legislation needs to be discussed and created regarding the road safety, cyber security and data privacy. Consumers need to be sure that they are safe not only on the roads, but that their information is safe from potential hackers. There will need to be industry wide standards for the testing of autonomous vehicles in order to prove that these vehicles will keep the passenger safe and it can resolve any potential issue that could occur (Dimitrakopoulos et al., 2021).

Currently, Norway has already implemented framework conditions for testing these vehicles, while Italy has already passed a law for these tests. This shows that some countries are taking steps in the right direction towards getting autonomous vehicles on the roads safely (Dimitrakopoulos et al., 2021).

Level 3 autonomous vehicles are definitely on their way to coming onto the market. However, it will be quite a while before Level 4 and 5 vehicles are available to the public. Not necessarily due to a lack of technological capacity, but more from a lack of security and legislation protecting individual’s security.

Machine learning is a subset of AI (artificial intelligence). Which is the process that allows a computer to imitate the way humans are able to learn and gather information (Heath, 2020). • Image recognition (Guerrouj, 2020) – Being able to determine what object is inside of a photo. An example of image recognition in use is Google lens. Which is an application that is able to provide information of an object captured in a photo.

What does it do?

Cloud computing as officially defined by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) reads: “Cloud computing is a model for enabling ubiquitous, convenient, on-demand network access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (e.g., networks, servers, storage, applications, and services) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with minimal management effort or service provider interaction” (Mell & Grance, 2011). What does this mean to the home computer user? Well cloud computing is the ability to access the internet anywhere, anytime and use the resources, data and applications you need without the requirement of understanding the technology.

Cloud computing has taken the traditional data centre and on-premises corporate functionality and made these accessible, financially and physically, to any user.

On-demand self service enables consumers to provision or de-provision services offered by cloud vendors as and when required to meet consumer needs, without the delay of going through the provider. Broad network access is the connectivity to the services being consumed, this is usually through high bandwidth internet services into a cloud provider, however, could also be on a Local Area Network (LAN) in the event of a private cloud. Resource pooling or multi-tenancy provides financial benefits to consumers by allowing the same hardware resources to be accessed by multiple consumers, reducing costs to the consumer. Rapid elasticity takes on-demand self service and enables the growth or reduction of cloud services based on demand or pre-determined events. Measured service is the way that the cloud provider determines the cost for a defined period. Cloud services are a “pay for what you use” basis, this means that the other four characteristics will determine the costs for the consumer, this could be an annual, monthly, weekly, daily, hourly, minute or second charge.

Traditional corporate ICT systems involve large investment in hardware (servers, storage and network), data centre services to host the hardware or configuration of on-premises rooms to accommodate the hardware and the personnel to manage and maintain the hardware. When you have the hardware assembled and managed you then need teams to provision and maintain the applications that the corporation utilises and platform(s) that the application reside on.

The service models with cloud computing take these three areas and reduce the management overheads with the associated service offered. Cloud providers use secure data centres to provision hardware, consumers are then able to use this hardware as IaaS, negating the requirement to procure hardware. This is extremely advantageous for small and start-up companies that do not have the financial equity to make an investment in hardware. With IaaS consumers do not get access to hardware this is fully maintained by the cloud provider and the consumer is responsible to maintain the platform and applications required. PaaS further reduces the management overhead for companies by providing the platform, or operating environment required for the applications the company uses. PaaS is used by application development teams to reduce the overhead of providing a consistent and up-to-date environment and smaller companies to remove the ICT management overheads. SaaS provides applications over the internet, usually for a subscription fee. SaaS applications can be used in corporate environments, smaller companies and individuals. SaaS has enabled expensive and complex to manage applications to be available to all consumers, including individuals for education and learning.

Cloud computing has reached into all aspects of life, early adopters have been education, social media, entertainment, navigation, personal storage, online shopping and online gaming/gambling. The current cloud computing concept has been around since the early 2000’s, but what's ultimately driving the public cloud adoption are this ease-of-use, flexibility. More control around what you consume, and when you consume it, and how you consume it (McDonough, 2018)

What is the likely impact?

As more services move to a cloud based delivery model the consumer will need to attain faster and more reliable internet connections. The cloud is driving mobility and flexible working conditions, companies are now able to make remote and collaborative working better for employees. The employment market has changed to enable companies to employ staff with the right skillsets not necessarily in the immediate vicinity, but now anywhere in the world. This flows into the local community with greater investment in small retail businesses that might historically rely on seasonal trade or lower income community members.

Companies are changing the way that they deliver services to employees, 81 percent of companies with 1,000 employees or more have a multi-platform strategy. The number is to rise to more than 90 percent by 2024 (Saleem, 2020). As the understanding of what the cloud can offer grows, companies are more willing to embrace the change and put more services online.

Some growth areas that have seen changes due to the cloud are quantum computing, automation and Internet of Things (IoT). Quantum computers are now available via cloud services and are enabling research labs and think tanks to process complex algorithm calculations and process massive datasets in quick time. Financial institutions can process transactions faster, encryption algorithms are able to be processed faster meaning transactions are more secure. The cloud has seen an increase in automation for business processes, support systems are more stable and consistent allowing businesses to reduce errors and increase productivity.

Today just about anything can be connected to the internet, these devices comprise the IoT. These could be refrigerators, televisions, washing machines, wearable devices, lightbulbs, house locks or security cameras, these connected devices are enabling people to be more efficient and safer. The IoT devices need to be registered to a cloud service and this cloud service is how the consumer can interact with them. This could be in a private cloud in the personal home or in a managed online service with data analytics being conducted. This online capability is what has seen some security issues raised regarding IoT as some services have not had adequate security procedures built into the offering and devices and have been remotely controlled or personal data has been exposed.

Due to the way that traditional IT systems were built and supported there has not been a downturn in employment with the uptake of cloud services. Roles of the workforce have changed however, and employees are having to learn new skills to adapt to the changing landscape.

Project 'Ice Scream'

Artwork for Ice Scream is designed by our very own Adam Maiorana

Overview

Ice Scream is a 2D platformer developed with Unity. The game is focused around our main protagonist, a frightened ice cream who was accidentally dropped by the local ice cream man and now needs to make it back to the fridge before he melts. The world is a very hostile place for our poor little ice cream. The hot sun is beaming down towards earth and is rapidly melting our hero. In order to survive they must navigate through the environment by conquering increasingly difficult obstacles. They also have to contend with the hungry neighbourhood children and their vicious dogs, who want nothing more than to eat him up. During this journey they will meet other weird and wonderful characters that could help or hinder our heroes journey back to the safety of the fridge. Given the scope of the project - a realistic goal would be to achieve a playable demo or alpha build rather than a fully developed game that is ready to ship. Demonstrating the games core mechanics and features on a smaller scale. Due to time constraints and our overall skill level, I think this would be the best approach.

Description

The main feature of the game would be that it is a 2D platformer. The Player would move from point A to Point B to progress to the level. The player would control the characters movements through the level, avoiding hazards and obstacles with the goal of reaching safety before you melt! The levels would have puzzle-like elements that become harder as you progress to keep them interesting and challenging. The main element of danger would be from the sun’s rays. Whenever the character is in direct sunlight, it will damage the player's health. Too much sun and the player will die. That's where shade comes in. Shade would play a big part in the game's mechanics as it would be the only place the player could stand without taking damage from the sun. Shaded areas would be limited to keep the level challenging. Along with the dangers, there would also be elements in the game that would help the player achieve his goal. For example, collecting ice cubes would slow down the melting mechanic by replenishing the player's health and liquid chocolate would form a protective helmet that would protect the character from the sun's rays or other hazards. The art style would be colourful and vibrant. The world is filled with bizarre and wonderful characters. Below is a rough sketch of the main character. Giving a good indication of the art style.

Tools & Technologies

Engine

The engine is a crucial piece of software that is needed to create a game. It consists of multiple components, all operating together. Below I will outline some of the main components of an engine.
Physics Engine: The engine will have its own set of rules to generate physical laws within the environment such as gravity and also gives you the ability to manipulate these forces.
Rendering Engine: Generates images from either a 2D or 3D model.
Audio Engine: This offers you the framework to add and edit audio.
There are a number of game engines available out there. The majority of them are free for personal use but when it comes to
revenue and publishing that's where cost becomes a factor. Below I have listed some of the more well known ones and compared them.

Unity

Developed by Unity Technologies

Pros


- Unity offers the option to develop both 2D and 3D projects.
- Develop for multiple platforms such as PC, Mobile, Xbox, Playstation etc.
- Beginner friendly.
- Free to publish as long as you are not generating over $100,000 annually.
- Active community and lots of tutorials on youtube.
- Asset store containing lots of free assets.
- Student developers pack is available for free.
- Uses C# scripting. Considered a beginner friendly language.

Cons


- The whole Unity package is quite large in size, around 9GB.
- They don't offer any grants to indie developers, while other game engine developers do.
- Once you exceed the $100,000 annual revenue, a subscription or license is required.

Unreal

Developed by Epic Games

Pros


- Considered the more powerful engine when it comes to high quality graphics. A lot of AAA studios use this engine because of this reason.
- Open-source is considered an advantage because it makes development easier.
- Unreal engine has great performance and uses less memory and resources.
- Has a wide range of built in tools that would require coding in other engines. Ie Visual editors.

Cons


- Once you begin making money on your product you pay a 5% royalty on the amounts after the first $3000 per product per calendar quarter.
- Small but more curated asset store.
- Not suitable for small games. Typically used for studios with big budgets.
- Smaller community
- Uses C++ which is considered a difficult language to learn.

Gamemaker studio 2

Developed by YoYo Games

Pros


- Good for quickly prototyping a project
- User friendly interface
- Large community offering lots of tutorials
- Good documentation

Cons


- Expensive. You need to pay per platform and on top of that you need to pay $800 annually for each platform you publish on.
- Uses its own language called GML which is known for teaching bad coding habits, along with not being able to transfer those skills to other languages.
- Does not have great support from the developers as they are a small team. Bug fixes can take a very long time to be ironed out.
- Reported as being unstable.

After careful consideration - Unity is still the preferred Engine for developing this project. As outlined above, Unity offers a lot for free.
It has a great community, lots of tutorials and support, is multi platform and even offers free high quality assets for students.
It seems like the most logical choice and the pros far outweigh the cons.

Cost & Funding

Undertaking game development can be quite costly if you don’t already own some of the basic equipment required such as a modern computer. As the computer itself would be your biggest expense, the other requirements can be quite inexpensive or have free alternatives.

As for funding A prototype would need to be developed in order to generate funding. This would mean taking the demo and making it a completely polished, bit size chuck of the game, that is the best representation of the product so that it can be sent to publishers to try and secure funding for full development. By sending the prototype to publishers you could get some great early feedback and see if there is any interest in the project. If there is interest, it would mean that you are onto something that publishers believe they/you could make money from. This is also a great way of seeing if what you are working on is worth developing further. Even small games can take years to complete, so by getting feedback early on it could save you years of development on a project that no one is interested in. An alternative to publisher funding, you could start a crowdfunding page to generate revenue. By offering contributors different rewards tiers depending on how much they contribute to the project. This demonstrates that if your game captures interest within the gaming community, there is the potential to generate enough funding to complete the project.
